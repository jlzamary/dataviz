Map of Canada's Indigenous Populations

The First nation and Métis peoples are more concentrated in the southern Canadian Provinces while the Inuit people are much more concentrated in the northern and eastern portions of the Country. There are two provinces that do have a majority indigenous population. In both the Northwest Territories and Nunavut the population is 51% and 86% indigenous respectfully. While these regions have larger indigenous populations, their overall population is smaller compared to the more densely populated urban regions of the south.

The Inuit mainly inhabit Nunavut and areas of northern and eastern Canada. The First nation and Métis peoples are more concentrated in the southern Canadian Provinces with their largest populations in Ontario.

Scatter Plot Comparing Food Insecurity to Poor Mental Health

The first visible trend is that across all categories, indigenous peoples tend to assess themselves with higher levels of poor mental health compared to the non-indigenous peoples. Across indigenous tribes, the Métis remain grouped together, while the First Nations have a much greater spread, from some experiencing little poor mental health to others lying on the other side of the spectrum. The Inuit remain consistent at around 10% for all categories. Between sexes, females tend to rank themselves as having slightly higher poor mental health than males.

Similar trends from the previous question seem to apply here. Across genders, indigenous peoples tend to experience more food insecurity when compared to non-indigenous peoples. Across tribes, the Métis and First Nations experience similar levels, around 10-30 percent, with the First Nations seeing more variation in the data. The Inuit are the outliers here, reporting very high food insecurity at over 70%. Regarding gender, the general trend seems to be females experience slightly more insecurity. However, when it comes to the Inuit, females actually experience less food insecurity, though the percentage remains much higher than for other groups.

The relationship in the plot between both sexes appears to have a positive linear trend, indicating that higher food insecurity rates are correlated to poor mental health. The female plot sees a similar trend, while the male plot has a much less significant linear trend. Overall I would interpret this as communities with less access to food typically are more marginalized and experience a worse lifestyle. Because of this, they tend to have higher rates of poor mental health status. The increase in female reporting suggests that while Canada is a more modern country, they still face gender-based discrimination, which negatively impacts the female population more than the males.

Distribution of Highly Populated Indigenous Regions

The three provinces here differ greatly in non-indigenous versus indigenous populations. Nanavut has a large (86%) indigenous population mainly composed of the Inuit. However, the province's overall population is significantly less compared to the other regions. The Northwest Territories has a very balanced population (51% indigenous) with the majority of the indigenous population being made up of the First Nation tribes. Lastly, Manitoba has the largest overall population and the highest percentage of non-indigenous people (82%), while the percentages of Inuit, First Nations, and Métis peoples are low.
